Common Species & Locations

Brown Trout

Identifying a native Idaho brown trout involves noting several key characteristics. Brown trout typically have a brown or golden-brown coloration with darker spots scattered across their bodies, which often have a halo of lighter color around them. These spots are more concentrated towards the upper body and back, with fewer spots on the lower sides. Additionally, brown trout have a slightly more robust and elongated body compared to other trout species, with a squared-off tail. Brown trout generally occupy specific types of habitats that suit their need for cooler, well-oxygenated water. They are most commonly found in fast-moving streams, rivers, and high-oxygenated areas. These environments provide the necessary conditions for their survival, including abundant food sources and suitable spawning grounds. It is less likely to catch brown trout in slow-moving bodies of water, as these do not typically provide the same levels of oxygen and habitat complexity that brown trout prefer. By targeting these dynamic and oxygen-rich environments, anglers can increase their chances of successfully locating and catching brown trout.

Rainbow Trout

Identifying a native Idaho rainbow trout involves noting several key characteristics. Rainbow trout are known for their striking coloration, which includes a broad pink to red stripe running along their lateral line from gills to tail. Their bodies are generally silver to greenish in color, covered with small black spots that extend onto their dorsal fins and tail. The coloration can vary depending on their environment and diet, but the distinctive red stripe and the speckling are reliable identification markers. Rainbow trout in Idaho are mostly introduced by fish farms, which stock them in various bodies of water to support recreational fishing. These farm-raised fish are well-adapted to the local conditions and provide a popular target for anglers. Rainbow trout generally occupy specific types of habitats that suit their need for cooler, well-oxygenated water. They are most commonly found in fast-moving streams, rivers, and other high-oxygenated areas. These environments provide the necessary conditions for their survival, including abundant food sources and suitable spawning grounds. It is less likely to catch rainbow trout in slow-moving bodies of water, as these do not typically provide the same levels of oxygen and habitat complexity that rainbow trout prefer. By targeting these dynamic and oxygen-rich environments, anglers can increase their chances of successfully locating and catching rainbow trout.

Cutthroat Trout

Identifying a native Idaho cutthroat trout involves noting several key characteristics. Cutthroat trout are named for the distinctive red or orange slash marks on the underside of their lower jaw. Their bodies typically have a golden or yellowish tint, with dark spots concentrated towards the tail and fewer spots on the head. These spots are irregular in shape and size, giving the cutthroat trout a unique and easily recognizable appearance. Cutthroat trout in Idaho generally occupy specific types of habitats that suit their need for cooler, well-oxygenated water. They are most commonly found in fast-moving streams, rivers, and other high-oxygenated areas. These environments provide the necessary conditions for their survival, including abundant food sources and suitable spawning grounds. It is less likely to catch cutthroat trout in slow-moving bodies of water, as these do not typically provide the same levels of oxygen and habitat complexity that cutthroat trout prefer. By targeting these dynamic and oxygen-rich environments, anglers can increase their chances of successfully locating and catching cutthroat trout. It is important to note that cutthroat trout are illegal to harvest in Idaho. This regulation is in place to protect the species and ensure their populations remain healthy and sustainable. Anglers must practice catch and release when fishing for cutthroat trout, handling them carefully to minimize stress and injury before releasing them back into the water. By adhering to these guidelines, anglers contribute to the conservation of this valuable native species.

Largescale Sucker

Identifying a native Idaho largescale sucker involves noting several key characteristics. Largescale suckers have a robust, elongated body with a broad head and a downturned mouth designed for bottom feeding. Their coloration is generally olive to brown on the back, fading to a lighter, yellowish underside. They have large scales, especially noticeable on the lower half of their body, which gives them their name. Largescale suckers typically inhabit slow-moving streams, rivers, and lakes where they feed on algae, detritus, and small invertebrates. Unlike fast-moving water preferred by trout, these environments suit the sucker's bottom-feeding habits. Despite their different habitat preferences, largescale suckers are an integral part of Idaho's aquatic ecosystems. It is crucial to note that largescale suckers should not be removed from the water. Unfortunately, they are often confused with the invasive common carp, leading some people to catch them and leave them on the bank to die. This practice is harmful and unjustified, as largescale suckers have every right to be in the water as trout do. While they are not considered good eating and are occasionally used as bait, they play a vital role in maintaining the ecological balance of their habitats. Anglers should handle largescale suckers with care, ensuring they are promptly and safely returned to the water if caught. By respecting this native species and recognizing their importance, anglers contribute to the health and diversity of Idaho's waterways. It's essential to correctly identify and protect largescale suckers, promoting sustainable fishing practices and preserving the natural environment for future generations.

Bass *Largemouth*

Identifying a native Idaho largemouth bass involves noting several key characteristics. Largemouth bass are easily recognized by their broad, horizontal mouths that extend past their eyes when closed. They have a greenish to olive coloration with dark, horizontal stripes along their sides. Their bodies are robust and streamlined, built for powerful swimming and predatory behavior. Largemouth bass typically inhabit warm, slow-moving waters such as lakes, ponds, and sluggish streams. They prefer areas with abundant vegetation, submerged logs, and other structures that provide cover and ambush points for their prey. These environments offer the ideal conditions for largemouth bass, which are top predators in their habitats, feeding on smaller fish, insects, and amphibians. It is important to note that largemouth bass should not be removed from the water unless they are intended for consumption. While they are popular among anglers for their sport and the challenge they present, largemouth bass play a crucial role in maintaining the ecological balance of their habitats. They help control populations of smaller fish and contribute to the overall health of the aquatic ecosystem. Anglers targeting largemouth bass should practice catch and release whenever possible to ensure sustainable populations. If an angler does choose to keep a bass, it should be done following local regulations and guidelines to avoid overharvesting. By handling largemouth bass carefully and respecting their role in the ecosystem, anglers can help preserve these valuable fish for future generations. Additionally, largemouth bass are not only prized for their sport but are also excellent table fare when properly prepared. They provide a delicious and nutritious option for those who enjoy cooking their catch. Ensuring that fishing practices remain sustainable and responsible will help maintain healthy populations of largemouth bass, benefiting both the environment and the fishing community.

Bass *Smallmouth*

Identifying a native Idaho smallmouth bass involves noting several key characteristics. Smallmouth bass have a more streamlined and elongated body compared to their largemouth counterparts. They are typically bronze to brownish-green in color with darker vertical bands along their sides, which distinguish them from the horizontal stripes of the largemouth bass. Their mouth is smaller and does not extend past the eyes when closed, hence the name "smallmouth." Smallmouth bass generally inhabit cooler, clearer, and faster-moving waters than largemouth bass. They are commonly found in rivers, streams, and rocky lakes where the water is well-oxygenated. These environments provide ideal conditions for smallmouth bass, which prefer rocky substrates, gravel beds, and areas with moderate to strong currents. They feed on a diet of smaller fish, crayfish, and insects, thriving in habitats that support their predatory nature. It is important to note that smallmouth bass should not be removed from the water unless intended for consumption within legal limits. These fish are a valuable part of Idaho's aquatic ecosystems, contributing to the ecological balance by controlling populations of smaller fish and invertebrates. While smallmouth bass are prized for their fighting spirit and are a popular target for sport fishing, sustainable practices must be observed to maintain healthy populations. Anglers targeting smallmouth bass should practice catch and release whenever possible, ensuring these fish are returned to their habitats unharmed. If an angler decides to keep a smallmouth bass, it should be done following local regulations and size limits to prevent overfishing. Handling smallmouth bass carefully and respecting their role in the ecosystem helps preserve this species for future generations. Additionally, smallmouth bass are known for their excellent table fare when properly prepared, providing a delicious and nutritious option for those who enjoy cooking their catch. By adhering to sustainable fishing practices and respecting local regulations, anglers can enjoy the thrill of catching smallmouth bass while ensuring their continued presence in Idaho's waters. This approach benefits both the environment and the fishing community, promoting a healthy and vibrant ecosystem.

Channel Catfish

Identifying a native Idaho channel catfish involves noting several key characteristics. Channel catfish have a smooth, scaleless body that is typically bluish-gray or silvery in color, with a distinctive forked tail and a series of dark spots along their sides, particularly in younger fish. Their heads are broad and flat, with prominent whisker-like barbels around their mouths, which they use to sense food in murky waters. Channel catfish generally inhabit warm, slow-moving waters such as rivers, lakes, and reservoirs. They prefer areas with muddy or sandy bottoms, where they can forage for food. These environments provide ideal conditions for channel catfish, which are bottom dwellers and feed on a varied diet of fish, insects, crustaceans, and plant material. They are particularly active during the night and are known for their strong sense of smell and taste, which helps them locate food in turbid waters. It is important to note that channel catfish should not be removed from the water unless intended for consumption within legal limits. These fish play a valuable role in Idaho's aquatic ecosystems, contributing to the ecological balance by feeding on various organisms and helping to control populations of other species. While channel catfish are popular among anglers for their size and the challenge they present, sustainable practices must be observed to maintain healthy populations. Anglers targeting channel catfish should practice catch and release whenever possible, ensuring these fish are returned to their habitats unharmed. If an angler decides to keep a channel catfish, it should be done following local regulations and size limits to prevent overfishing. Handling channel catfish carefully and respecting their role in the ecosystem helps preserve this species for future generations. Additionally, channel catfish are known for their excellent table fare, providing a delicious and nutritious option for those who enjoy cooking their catch. By adhering to sustainable fishing practices and respecting local regulations, anglers can enjoy the thrill of catching channel catfish while ensuring their continued presence in Idaho's waters. This approach benefits both the environment and the fishing community, promoting a healthy and vibrant ecosystem.

Sturgeon

Identifying a native Idaho white sturgeon involves noting several key characteristics. White sturgeon are among the largest freshwater fish in North America, characterized by their elongated, cylindrical bodies and a row of bony plates, or scutes, running along their back and sides. Their color ranges from gray to pale olive, and they have a pointed snout with four barbels located near their mouth, which they use to sense food. White sturgeon generally inhabit large, deep rivers and reservoirs. They prefer slow-moving waters with sandy or muddy bottoms where they can forage for a diverse diet of fish, crustaceans, and other aquatic organisms. These ancient fish are bottom dwellers and can live for many decades, growing to impressive sizes, which makes them a prized catch among sport anglers. However, new anglers should NOT target white sturgeon until they have thoroughly researched and acquired the proper gear. Catching these fish for sport is legal, but improper gearing is not and can cause significant harm to the fish. According to Idaho Fish and Game regulations, anglers fishing for white sturgeon are required to use barbless hooks, a sliding swivel device to secure a weight, and a lighter test line to secure the weight to the sliding swivel or other device. These regulations help ensure that the fish can be safely caught and released. Furthermore, it is crucial to keep white sturgeon submerged at all times, as it is unlawful to remove their heads from the water. All hooks and equipment should be removed carefully. In the case of a foul hook, it should be removed quickly, and the fish should be released immediately. If the fish swallows the equipment, the line should be cut as close to the lodged equipment as possible before releasing the fish quickly and safely. Anglers must practice responsible and sustainable fishing when targeting white sturgeon to ensure their conservation and continued presence in Idaho's waters. By adhering to these guidelines and regulations, anglers contribute to the preservation of this majestic species, supporting both the environment and the fishing community. Proper preparation, respect for the fish, and compliance with regulations are essential for a successful and ethical sturgeon fishing experience.

Locations

Lorenzo Boat Ramp, located in Ammon, Idaho, is a prime fishing spot that offers a diverse and rewarding experience for anglers. This location features a convenient boat ramp, allowing easy access for those who prefer to fish from a boat. Additionally, the area boasts a rocky riverbank that you can walk along, providing ample opportunities for shore fishing as you explore up and down the river for miles. Fishermen at Lorenzo Boat Ramp can expect to catch a variety of fish species, including rainbow trout, brown trout, cutthroat trout, largescale sucker, and, in some spots, catfish and bass. The fast-moving waters are ideal for using spoons and spinners, which are particularly effective in these conditions. The river's swift current provides the oxygen-rich environment that trout thrive in, making it an excellent spot for targeting these species. As you navigate the river, you will encounter areas with deep pools where trout tend to gather. These pools offer prime opportunities for catching larger fish as they rest and feed in the slower-moving water. The varied habitat along the river, with its mix of fast currents and deep pools, creates an ideal environment for both trout and other species. For anglers interested in largescale suckers, the rocky and sandy riverbeds provide a perfect habitat for these bottom dwellers. Additionally, certain spots along the river are known to harbor catfish and bass, adding to the diverse fishing opportunities available at this location. Overall, Lorenzo Boat Ramp in Ammon, Idaho, is a versatile and productive fishing spot. Whether you prefer fishing from a boat or walking along the riverbank, you will find numerous opportunities to catch a variety of fish species in a beautiful and dynamic setting. The combination of fast-moving water and deep pools makes it an exciting and rewarding destination for anglers of all skill levels.

Extra Tip!

This location is really good for Spinners and Kastmasters, there are some sections where bait works, but Lures work better here.

Beaver Dick Campground, located in Rexburg, Idaho, is a popular fishing destination known for its scenic beauty and diverse fishing opportunities. This location offers anglers the chance to catch a variety of fish species, including brown trout, catfish, and largescale suckers. Although it is rare, some anglers have also reported catching sturgeon in these waters, adding an element of excitement to the fishing experience. The campground is situated along the river, providing easy access to the water for both shore fishing and wading. The river's flow and structure create an ideal habitat for brown trout, with plenty of hiding spots and feeding areas. The fast currents and deeper sections of the river are perfect for using spoons and spinners, which are particularly effective in attracting brown trout. Catfish are also commonly caught in the slower-moving, deeper parts of the river. These areas provide the perfect environment for catfish to thrive, offering plenty of food and shelter. Anglers targeting catfish often use bait that can sit on the bottom, taking advantage of the catfish's bottom-feeding habits. Largescale suckers can be found in the rocky and sandy sections of the river, where they forage for food along the riverbed. These fish are a common catch at Beaver Dick Campground, providing a different challenge for anglers. Despite its popularity, securing a good fishing spot at Beaver Dick Campground can be challenging due to the high number of visitors. The campground is a favorite among locals and tourists alike, so it's important to arrive early to secure a prime fishing location. Overall, Beaver Dick Campground in Rexburg, Idaho, is a fantastic fishing spot that offers a variety of opportunities for catching brown trout, catfish, and largescale suckers. While sturgeon catches are rare, the possibility adds to the allure of this popular destination. With its beautiful surroundings and diverse fishing conditions, Beaver Dick Campground remains a top choice for anglers seeking a rewarding and enjoyable fishing experience.

Extra Tip!

This location has deeper, semi-fast moving water. So make sure you have heavy lures, or utilize bottom rigs such as baits.

Targhee National Forest, specifically the section located in Idaho, is a renowned destination for anglers seeking a remote and rewarding fishing experience. This area is known for its pristine waters and abundant fish populations, making it a prime spot for catching rainbow trout, brown trout, and cutthroat trout. While there have been occasional reports of sturgeon catches, these instances are quite rare. Fishing in Targhee National Forest requires a bit of effort, as many of the best spots are not easily accessible and require hiking through mild to difficult terrain. This effort is well worth it, as the remote nature of these locations often results in some of the best fishing opportunities in the region. The secluded waters provide an ideal habitat for trout, with fewer disturbances from human activity, allowing the fish to grow larger and more abundant. Rainbow trout in these waters are known for their vibrant colors and vigorous fights, making them a favorite target for anglers. Brown trout, with their distinctive spots and robust bodies, offer a challenging and rewarding catch. Cutthroat trout, recognized by the red or orange slashes under their jaws, are also commonly found and provide an exciting addition to the fishing experience. For those willing to undertake the hike, the remote fishing spots in Targhee National Forest offer unparalleled tranquility and the chance to connect deeply with nature. These areas are less crowded and provide a more serene fishing environment compared to the easily accessible locations, which can become quite crowded. However, there are more accessible spots within the forest that are popular and can attract a crowd, especially during peak fishing seasons. Anglers should plan accordingly, either by arriving early or by exploring the more remote areas to ensure a peaceful and productive fishing trip. Overall, Targhee National Forest in Idaho is a top-tier fishing destination for those seeking adventure and high-quality angling. With its stunning natural beauty and rich fish populations, it offers a truly unique and fulfilling experience for dedicated anglers. Whether you're after rainbow trout, brown trout, or cutthroat trout, the effort to reach these remote spots is rewarded with some of the best fishing conditions in the region.

This has some fast moving water, and is mostly shallow. So take advantage of semi-heavy spinners, or using bait. There are some section with deep pools with slow moving water, so keep that in mind too!
